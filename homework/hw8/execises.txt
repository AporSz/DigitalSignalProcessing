2. Define poles and zeros in the context of the Laplace transform. How do they influence the behavior of a system?

The system's behavior is described by its transfer function H(s) = N(s) / D(s). The zeros are the roots of N(s) and the poles are
the roots of D(s). The poles determine the system's stability (if they are on the left side of the s plane then the system is stable,
if they are on the right side the system is unstable, if the pole is on the imaginary axis then the system is marginally stable). Zeros
can block specific frequencies, and they determine the amplitude and phase of the response.

4. Explain why all-pole analogue filters can only be low-pass or band-pass.

All pole filter: H(s) = K / ((s-p1) * (s-p2) * ... * (s-pn))
As s approaches infinity, H(s) approaches 0. Thus an all pole filter will block high frequencies. It can't be a high pass filter.
It can't be a notch filter either because it blocks high filters. It can be a band pass filter if the poles are close to the imaginary axis.

5. What kind of filter does the transfer function H(s) = s / (s+1) represent? Argue it using words instead of calculations.

It has a zero at 0 so it blocks low frequencies. The limit of H(s) is 1 as s tends to infinity, so it lets high frequencies pass.
It is a high pass filter.

6. What is a Bode plot, and how is it used to analyze continuous-time systems? What are the key components of a Bode plot?

It plots, on a logarithmic axis, the frequencies against the system's gain (output magnitude / input magnitude) measured in deciBells.
It also plots the frequencies (still logarithmic axis) against the phase in degrees.
It is used to analyze filters and to tell easily what type of filter we're working with. It is also used for stability analysis and it can detect resonance too.

7. Explain how to interpret the gain and phase plots in a Bode diagram.

Gain plot: At 0 dB we have the baseline for the amplitude. Above 0 dB we have increase in gain, below it, we have decrease of gain. At -3 dB we have the cutoff frequency.
The slope can tell us about how aggressive a filter is.
Phase plot: 0 degrees: the output and input waves line up. Below 0 degrees we have lagging behind the input (poles cause phase lag). Above 0 degrees we have anticipation of
input (zeros cause phase lead). At -180 degrees the output is inverted compared to the input.

10. What is the z-transform, and how does it differ from the Fourier transform?

It is the discrete time equivalent of the Laplace transform. The DTFT is a special case of z-transform: it's kernel function is z^(-n), where z = r * e ^ (j * omega).
This allows the analys of divergent signals since the standard FT doesn't allow non-convergent signals.

11. Explain the region of convergence (ROC) for the z-transform. Why is it important?

The Region of Convergence (ROC) is the specific set of points in the complex $z$-plane where the z-transform summation mathematically converges to
a finite value. It is crucial because a single algebraic expression can represent completely different time-domain signals depending entirely on its ROC.
Furthermore, analyzing where the ROC sits relative to the unit circle instantly tells engineers whether a digital system is causal and stable.

12. How does the z-transform of the convolution of two sequences relate to the individual z-transforms of the two sequences?

The z-transform of the convolution of two discrete-time sequences is equal to the algebraic product of their individual z-transforms.
This fundamental relationship, written as Z{x[n] * h[n]} = X(z)H(z), is the cornerstone of digital signal processing.
It allows engineers to bypass complex time-domain convolution entirely, replacing it with basic algebraic multiplication in the frequency domain.

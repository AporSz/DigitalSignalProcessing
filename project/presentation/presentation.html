Digital Signal Processing | Covasna, Romania

Welcome to the presentation. Today we are presenting the measurements and modeling of carbon dioxide dynamics within a natural mofette (gas vent) located in Covasna, Romania. We'll explore the experimental remote sensing setup and dive deep into the digital signal processing (DSP) techniques used to uncover diurnal, seasonal, and environmental patterns in the gas emissions.

Tectonic & Volcanic Activity

Geological Setting

  • Volcanic Arc: Youngest volcanic chain in Europe, active with post-volcanic degassing.
  • Ciomadul Volcano: Youngest regional volcanic center (~30k years ago), retaining deep magma heat.
  • Tectonic Junction: Intersection of three micro-plates drives deep crustal faulting.
  • CO₂ Origin: Subduction slab detachment in the Vrancea zone feeds superficial mofettes.
Before looking at the gas sensors, let's look at the geological engine behind this mofette. Romania's Eastern Carpathians house the youngest volcanic arc in Europe, specifically the Ciomadul volcano which lies close to Covasna. The region sits at a complex tectonic junction where three plates meet. Tectonic slab-detachment in the deep Vrancea zone opens up pathways for deep, mantle-derived CO2 gas to ascend to the surface, creating natural gas pools or mofettes.

What is a Mofette?

Natural Venting

  • Volcanic vent emitting carbon dioxide (CO₂) and other dry gases.
  • Common in post-volcanic regions like the Eastern Carpathians (Romania).
  • Creates extreme microclimates and heavy gas pooling due to high CO₂ density relative to ambient air.
  • Serves as a natural laboratory for studying gas flow dynamics and carbon capture modeling.
Let's explain what a mofette is. A mofette is a dry volcanic vent that releases carbon dioxide. Because CO2 is heavier than air, it pools in ground depressions, creating extreme microclimates and asphyxiation zones. Here we see both a natural open mofette vent and a built/enclosed mofette monitoring station in Covasna. They act as perfect natural labs for studying gas flow and stratification.

Experimental Campaign & Input Data

Campaign Details

  • Location: Covasna, Romania.
  • Monitoring Period: Apr 8, 2022 to Nov 7, 2022.
  • Data Span: 200 days of active measurements.
  • Total Data points: 17.3 Million recordings.
  • Resolution: Continuous profiling at ~1 sample/second.

Input Data Files

  • Raw ASCII Text: `new_device_column1.txt` (~6 GB raw text data, alternating lines of timestamps and sensor values).
  • Processed CSV: `data.csv` (~6 GB standard table with headers).
The campaign in Covasna produced a massive amount of data. The raw output was a 6 GB text file containing 17.3 million samples. For analysis, this was converted into a standard 6 GB CSV table. During cleaning, invalid readings were filtered out, and the data was interpolated to a uniform 1-minute grid, giving us 200 days of active, clean time-series data.

Sensor Setup & Profiling

Custom Profiling Array

  • Main Stack: 20 vertical STC31 sensors spaced 5 cm apart (0 to 95 cm height) for profiling.
  • Side Tube: 4 additional STC31 sensors spaced 10 cm apart.
  • Meteorology: 2 BMP280 sensors (Barometric Pressure & Temperature) at top & bottom.
  • Humidity: 2 DHT11 humidity sensors at top & bottom.

Sensor Configuration

Sensor Group Type Count Spacing

The hardware setup was specifically built to capture vertical gas concentration gradients. Since CO2 is heavier than air, it builds up in stratified layers. By placing 20 thermal-conductivity-based STC31 sensors in a vertical stack spaced 5 cm apart, we can map the exact boundary layer where CO2 transitions to ambient air. Ambient pressure, humidity, and temperature sensors were installed at the top and bottom to monitor the environmental boundary conditions.

Exploratory Data Analysis

Raw Time Series Insights

  • Vertical Stratification: Lower stack sensors (0-30 cm) stay saturated at ~100% CO₂.
  • Active Boundary Zone: Middle sensors (40-60 cm) exhibit massive diurnal oscillations (daily cycles).
  • Emission Bursts: Sharp spikes in upper sensors correspond to brief high-intensity vents.
  • Seasonal Shift: Summer periods show wider daily fluctuation envelopes compared to spring/autumn.
Figure 1: 7-Month Raw CO₂ Profiling (20 Sensors)
Looking at the raw time series in Figure 1, we see the 20 vertical sensors. The bottom sensors are locked at nearly 100% CO2, indicating a permanent blanket of gas in the mofette. The upper sensors are mostly ambient air. The middle region (around 50cm, represented by sensor 10) is the critical dynamic boundary layer, showing massive daily oscillations. This area behaves like a breathing lung, expanding and contracting.

Frequency Domain: Welch's PSD

Power Spectral Density

  • Analyzed using Welch's Method (7-day window segment, 50% overlap).
  • Successfully isolates periodic signals from high-frequency sensor noise.
  • Reveals a highly dominant peak at the 24-hour period (diurnal cycle).
  • Secondary harmonics are visible at 12-hour and 8-hour intervals, indicating non-sinusoidal distortions.
Figure 2: Welch PSD of CO₂ (Sensor 10)
To mathematically confirm these cycles, we transformed the signal to the frequency domain. Using Welch's Power Spectral Density with a 7-day window to achieve good low-frequency resolution, we obtained the spectrum shown in Figure 2. The absolute dominant spike corresponds to exactly 24 hours. The presence of secondary peaks at 12 and 8 hours indicates that the daily cycle is not a pure sine wave, but has sharper, asymmetric transitions.

Direct Frequency Spectrum (FFT)

Fourier Transform Analysis

  • Calculated using the Fast Fourier Transform (FFT) on detrended data.
  • Signals are mapped to uniform 1-minute time grids via linear interpolation to fix sampling jitter.
  • Strong magnitude spike at f = 0.0417 cycles/hour (1/24 h⁻¹).
  • Proves the physical mechanism driving mofette gas expansion is strictly tied to solar/diurnal cycles.
Figure 3: FFT Magnitude Spectrum
Complementing the PSD, a direct FFT of the detrended 1-minute interpolated data was performed. In Figure 3, we see the raw magnitude spectrum. Again, the 24-hour cycle stands out massively at 0.0417 cycles per hour. The low-frequency region is highly resolved, confirming that diurnal forcing is the primary periodic engine of the system.

Seasonal Trends: Lowpass Filter

Lowpass Butterworth Filter

  • Designed a 3rd-order Butterworth Lowpass Filter.
  • Cutoff Period: 3 days (cutoff frequency ≈ 3.86 × 10⁻⁶ Hz).
  • Implemented using zero-phase filtering (`sosfiltfilt`) to prevent time lag shifts.
  • Removes all daily fluctuations, isolating the **long-term seasonal trend** (Spring → Summer → Autumn).
Figure 4: Raw CO₂ vs. 3-Day Lowpass Trend
To study the long-term patterns, we designed a Butterworth lowpass filter with a 3-day cutoff period. We used scipy's dual-pass `sosfiltfilt` function to ensure zero phase distortion, meaning the output is not shifted in time. Figure 4 shows the result: the red line isolates the seasonal base level. We see that the average CO2 level at 50 cm rises in the summer and drops in the autumn, correlating with ambient geological heating and regional barometric seasonal trends.

Isolating the Daily Cycle: Bandpass

Bandpass Butterworth Filter

  • Isolates the 24-hour oscillation by rejecting both low-frequency trends and high-frequency noise.
  • Passband Range: 18h to 30h periods (cutoff frequencies of 1.54 × 10⁻⁵ Hz to 9.26 × 10⁻⁶ Hz).
  • A zoomed 2-week window (Figure 5, bottom) reveals a clean, highly regular, sinusoidal diurnal breathing pattern.
Figure 5: Isolated Diurnal Cycle (Full & Zoomed)
Next, we wanted to see the pure diurnal cycle without seasonal trends. We designed a Butterworth bandpass filter centered on the 24-hour period, with cutoffs at 18 hours and 30 hours. In Figure 5, the top pane shows the isolated signal over the full 7 months. The bottom pane shows a 2-week zoom. The blue line represents the raw detrended data, and the green line is the bandpass output. It reveals an incredibly stable, regular wave.

Spectrogram: Time-Frequency View

Spectrogram Insights

  • Computed with a 7-day sliding window moving in 1-day steps.
  • Plots the strength of frequencies (Y-axis) against the 7-month calendar (X-axis).
  • The white dashed line indicates the **24-hour period**.
  • Shows that the 24h cycle is **highly active throughout the year**, but intensifies in late summer (Aug-Sep), indicating solar/temperature dependency.
Figure 6: 7-Month Spectrogram (6h to 72h Periods)
Is this 24-hour cycle constant, or does it change? To find out, we generated a spectrogram, shown in Figure 6. The horizontal axis is time (7 months), and the vertical axis is the period in hours. The color intensity represents energy. You can see a bright, continuous horizontal strip at 24 hours. The intensity is strongest in August and September, which indicates that summer conditions amplify the diurnal breathing amplitude of the mofette.

Drivers: Pressure & Temperature

Cross-Correlation Analysis

  • Barometric Pressure (Figure 7): Strong negative correlation (r = -0.42) at a lag of ~0 hours. Rising air pressure forces gas down into the vent; dropping pressure pulls gas out.
  • Ambient Temperature (Figure 8): Positive correlation (r = +0.28) at a lag of ~3 hours. Ambient temperature leads CO₂ gas expansion, indicating thermal drive.
Figure 7: X-Corr: CO₂ vs. Pressure
Figure 8: X-Corr: CO₂ vs. Temp
What environmental factors drive this breathing? We ran cross-correlation between CO2 and atmospheric pressure, and CO2 and ambient temperature. Pressure shows a strong negative correlation at 0 hours lag. When atmospheric pressure drops, it acts like a pump, drawing CO2 up and out. Ambient temperature shows a positive correlation peaking at a 3-hour lag. Sunlight warms the ground and air, causing thermal expansion that drives gas upward after a 3-hour thermal inertia delay.

Conclusions & Modeling Value

Key Scientific Findings

  • Confirmed **stratified CO₂ gas layering** in the mofette stack.
  • Established **barometric pressure** as the primary immediate physical driver of gas rise.
  • Identified a **diurnal 24h rhythm** driven by temperature cycles with a **3-hour thermal lag**.

Future Directions

  • Predictive Models: Training neural networks (LSTMs) or physical diffusion models using pressure/temperature inputs.
  • Safety Monitoring: Building alert systems for dangerous CO₂ levels based on barometric pressure forecasts.
  • Volcanic Activity Indicators: Tracking changes in mofette breathing rates to detect deep crustal shifts.
In conclusion, our study successfully mapped mofette dynamics using vertical profiling and digital signal processing. We confirmed that the mofette's "breathing" is driven directly by barometric pressure changes and diurnal temperature cycles. This data provides a solid foundation for predictive safety alert systems and geological volcanic monitoring.
